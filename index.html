Part I

Electric charge

Every material is built from atoms, and every atom carries three kinds of particles: protons (positive), electrons (negative), and neutrons (no charge). Electricity begins the moment electrons start to move. A material with electrons free to drift from atom to atom can carry electricity; a material that holds its electrons tightly cannot.

Part IImeasured in amperes (A)

Electric current

Current is the flow of charge through a material — the electrical equivalent of water flowing through a pipe. It only exists when there's a complete, unbroken path for charge to travel, which is why a circuit has to be closed to do anything at all.

One quirk worth knowing: electrons physically travel from the negative terminal toward the positive one, but by long-standing convention, engineers draw "conventional current" flowing the opposite way, from positive to negative.

Part IIImeasured in volts (V)

Voltage

Voltage is the push behind the flow — the electrical potential difference that gives charges a reason to move at all. No voltage, no current, no matter how good the conductor is.

AA battery≈ 1.5 V

Part IVmeasured in ohms (Ω)

Resistance

Every material resists the flow of current to some degree — resistance is that opposition. It's not automatically a bad thing; it's what makes toasters, kettles, and incandescent filaments work. Left uncontrolled, though, it shows up as three effects worth remembering:

Heat

  • Energy converts to heat as current pushes through resistance

Voltage drop

  • Some of the driving voltage is "used up" crossing the resistance

Energy loss

  • Especially significant over long transmission distances
Part Vmeasured in watts (W)

Electrical power

Power describes the rate of energy transfer — not how much energy something uses in total, but how fast it uses it. A higher wattage device draws more energy per second, which is why a heater feels different on your electric bill than an LED lamp.

Part VI

AC and DC electricity

Direct current flows one way, steadily. Alternating current reverses direction on a regular cycle. Toggle between them below to see the difference on the wire.

AC — current periodically reverses direction. Used in household outlets and national power grids.

Why grids use AC: transformers can step AC voltage up for long-distance transmission and back down for safe household use — something DC can't do nearly as efficiently. That single property is why AC, not DC, carries power across entire countries.

Part VII

Electrical safety

Electricity is only dangerous under one specific condition: when current finds a path through a human body. Every safety system below exists to prevent exactly that.

Grounding

Gives fault current a safe, low-resistance path to earth instead of through a person.

Fuses

A thin element that melts and breaks the circuit when current climbs too high.

Circuit breakers

Automatically disconnect a circuit the instant a fault is detected — and can be reset.
